DAGPap23: Detecting automatically generated scientific papers

Non-sensical machine-generated content is increasingly seen and being published in scientific papers. In this challenge, an iteration of a similar shared task held within last year’s workshop on Scholarly Document Processing, we continue exploring the state of the art in detecting automatically generated papers. In spite of seeing very high F1 score on the leaderboard of the 2022 version of the competition, the solutions are still very susceptible to data drift. As explored by one of the winning teams of last year’s DAGPAP competition, the models trained with the provided dataset failed to generalize well to a similar synthetic dataset, and thus the presented solutions are far from being mature enough to be utilized.

We also encourage contributions that aim to extend this dataset with other computer-generated scientific papers, or papers that propose valid metrics to assess automatically generated papers against those written by humans.

Context23: Contextualizing Scientific Figures and Tables

When making sense of results across many research papers on a topic, figures or tables of key results from the papers can serve as effective, information-dense summaries that can be compared/contrasted and synthesized with other results. However, to understand the results, key elements (e.g., measures, sample) need to be contextualized with associated methodological details, which are typically dispersed throughout the text, often far from the figure/table and from each other. In this shared task, we are interested in contextualizing scientific figures and tables, i.e., automatically retrieving and ranking snippets from the paper that are most needed to interpret their results, with the goal of making figures/tables more self-contained.
